In Western theory, knowledge builds power in the rethinking of how language (or discourse) works to encapsulate, even compose, subjects. The observation ranges from Michel Foucault's use of the term genealogy to Julia Kristeva and Roland Barthes' emphasis on breaking the conventions of language by re-reading the world of voices (see "geno-text") or, for instance, in the decomposition of music (see "geno-song"). A subject is a body and a thematic composition rooted in the language of the past (the archive) and the organization of the world (as a plurality of voices).
On the other hand, Marie Alohalani Brown, masterfully recovers voices of the past in the cataloguing of kanaka maoli narratives of Moʻo Akua--reptile water deities that pointed to larger moʻolelo (narrative historical structures) that fold the kanaka maoli pasts into the present. Her brilliant treatment draws heavily on native epistemological structures of organization known as helu:

Brown goes on to write of the ways "helu" seemed foreign to Western intepretations of 19th century Hawaiʻi and how these meticulous modes of classification help to organize similiarities and differences as connective nodes that exist across the Hawaiian language: for instance, where Moʻo, Moʻolelo, and Moʻopuna align. This point has been addressed in the past by public intellectuals like Akoni Akana and point to the increasing complexity of the digital archive as a tool of empowerment. This epistemological theme also underlies the more general nexus between academic scholarship and the political movement to reclaim land and water in Hawaiʻi.
